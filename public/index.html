Something is being lost quietly.

You ask an AI whether a supplement works. It answers. It sounds authoritative. It references no study you can verify, no number you can check. The actual record — 23 clinical trials, two meta-analyses, four industry-funded studies that should disqualify them — becomes one sentence. You accepted the sentence.

The data exists. It has always existed. The DOT publishes every flight delay, indexed and time-stamped. NHTSA files every crash, every recall, every fatality investigation. PubMed holds every study, including the ones that contradict the answer you just got.

But you didn't get the record. You got a translation. And something in the original doesn't survive the trip.

An AI translation isn't neutral. It is trained on what humans found interesting, which is not the same as what is true. It smooths contradiction. It presents confidence where the record shows conflict. It cannot tell you what it doesn't know, so it doesn't.

Some decisions shouldn't be filtered. Not the ones where you're putting your family in a car. Not the ones where you're picking an airline through a storm. Not the ones where a doctor already told you something and you need to know if the research agrees.

Open Primitive doesn't translate. It shows you the record. You read it. You decide.
